# Thesis and Dissertation Structure Guide for DH

## Overview

DH dissertations and theses occupy a unique structural space: they typically require both the methodological rigor expected in a technical study and the interpretive depth expected in a humanities dissertation. Conventions vary by institution, department, and committee, but the patterns below reflect common DH dissertation structures.

---

## Standard DH Dissertation Structure (5–7 Chapters)

### Chapter 1: Introduction
- Research question (dual-framed: interpretive + methodological)
- Significance: why this matters to the field(s)
- Overview of methodology and corpus
- Chapter map
- Typical length: 8,000–12,000 words

### Chapter 2: Literature Review
- Humanities scholarship on your object of study
- Technical DH scholarship on your methods
- The gap both bodies of literature leave open
- How your project bridges/fills the gap
- Typical length: 10,000–15,000 words

### Chapter 3: Data and Methods
- Corpus description: construction, scope, exclusions, biases
- OCR quality assessment (if applicable)
- Methods: computational techniques, tools, parameters
- Ethical considerations (rights, provenance, anonymization)
- Validation and reproducibility
- Typical length: 8,000–12,000 words

### Chapter 4: [First Empirical Chapter]
- First major finding or argument
- Computational results interpreted through humanistic lens
- Typical length: 12,000–18,000 words

### Chapter 5: [Second Empirical Chapter]
- Second major finding; may challenge or complicate Chapter 4
- Typical length: 12,000–18,000 words

### Chapter 6: [Third Empirical Chapter — if applicable]
- Extension, counter-argument, or alternative interpretation
- Typical length: 10,000–15,000 words

### Chapter 7: Conclusion
- Summary of argument across chapters
- What the DH approach made possible
- Limitations of the study
- Future research directions
- Typical length: 5,000–8,000 words

---

## MA Thesis Structure (3–4 Chapters, ~25,000–50,000 words)

1. Introduction + Literature Review (combined or separate)
2. Data and Methods
3. Analysis (can be 1–2 chapters)
4. Conclusion

## DH-Specific Chapter Considerations

### The Methods Chapter Challenge
A DH methods chapter must be legible to both technical and humanistic committee members. Strategies:
- Lead with the research question; end with how the method answers it
- Use a short "Methods Overview" for humanistic readers, followed by technical details
- Include a worked example (a single document or data point) to illustrate the method
- Cite the seminal method paper AND a DH application of it

### Handling OCR Quality in the Dissertation
If your corpus has known OCR issues, address them explicitly in Chapter 3:
1. Report OCR accuracy for each sub-corpus or collection
2. Describe any post-correction applied
3. State which analyses are affected by OCR noise and how
4. Cite the relevant literature (DHQ "Mining for the Meanings of a Murder"; DSH "Quantifying the impact of dirty OCR")

### Balancing Close and Distant Reading
A strong DH dissertation often pairs:
- Corpus-level computational analysis (Chapters 4–5)
- Close reading of selected representative or anomalous cases (within each chapter)
This "zoom out / zoom in" pattern is a recognized DH genre convention.

## Word Count Planning

For a 75,000-word dissertation:

| Chapter | Target Words |
|---|---|
| Introduction | 10,000 |
| Literature Review | 14,000 |
| Data and Methods | 11,000 |
| Empirical Ch. 1 | 16,000 |
| Empirical Ch. 2 | 14,000 |
| Conclusion | 8,000 |
| Bibliography | ~2,000 entries |
| **Total** | **75,000** |

Note: bibliography word counts vary; confirm with your institution whether bibliography counts toward the limit.
