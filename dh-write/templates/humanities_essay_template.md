## [Body — no required headers; use only if essential for navigation]

### Introduction (~10% of word count)

Open with:
- The primary source, text, or historical moment that anchors the argument
- The puzzle, tension, or interpretive problem
- A brief indication of the scholarly stakes
- The paper's central argument (usually in the last paragraph of the introduction, or in an explicit thesis sentence)

*Anti-pattern*: Opening with "Digital humanities is a growing field..." or any general statement that delays the argument.

---

### Background and Scholarly Context (~15–20% of word count)

Situate the argument in:
- Prior scholarly interpretations of the object of study
- The theoretical framework (if any) being deployed
- The gap this essay fills

This section demonstrates you know the conversation you're entering. It should not be a literature summary — it should be a map of the intellectual terrain that shows where this essay's argument fits.

---

### [Argumentative Sections — 2–4 sections, each advancing the claim] (~50–60% of word count)

Each section:
1. Opens with a topic sentence that advances the central argument (not just introduces a topic)
2. Presents evidence (quotation, paraphrase, description)
3. Analyzes the evidence closely (what does this language/structure/form do?)
4. Connects the analysis back to the central claim
5. Transitions to the next section's contribution

**For DH essays**: one section may present computational findings, but it should be framed in terms of the humanistic argument, not as a standalone technical report. Example: "The topic model's identification of [cluster] across 200 editorials suggests that..."

---

### Conclusion (~8–10% of word count)

The conclusion:
- **Does not** summarize what the essay said
- **Does** synthesize what the essay has established — what the reader can now see that they could not before
- States the contribution: why this reading matters for the field
- May gesture toward unresolved tensions or future questions (but sparingly)

*Template notes (delete before submission):*
- *In pure humanities essays, section headers are often absent. Use them only for very long essays (7,000+ words) or if venue conventions expect them.*
- *First person ("I argue," "this essay contends") is increasingly accepted in humanities journals; check venue conventions.*
- *Avoid beginning any paragraph with a quotation — provide context first.*
