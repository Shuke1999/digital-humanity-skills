## Problem Statement

[200–400 words]

What is the humanistic problem, question, or puzzle this project addresses? Situate it in its scholarly context. What has been argued? What remains unresolved? Why does this matter — to the field, to cultural understanding, to the interpretation of historical events?

---

## Significance

[200–300 words]

- **Scholarly significance**: What does this add to the field?
- **Broader significance**: What does this illuminate about culture, history, or language beyond specialist audiences?
- **DH significance**: What does the computational / digital approach make possible that prior scholarship could not? What is the methodological contribution?

---

## Research Question

[1–3 sentences]

State the research question with its dual framing:
- **Interpretive claim**: What will this project argue about the cultural/historical/literary object?
- **Methodological contribution**: What does the DH approach enable?

---

## Prior Scholarship

[300–500 words]

Review the relevant prior scholarship in two streams:
1. Humanistic scholarship on the topic/corpus/period
2. Computational DH scholarship using similar methods

State the gap that this project addresses.

---

## Corpus and Data

[200–400 words]

Describe the primary source corpus:
- What materials (newspapers, manuscripts, books, digital-born texts)?
- Which archives and collections?
- Approximate size (documents, pages, tokens)?
- Time period and geographic scope?
- Known quality issues (OCR accuracy, completeness, coverage gaps)?
- Rights and access?

---

## Methodology

[300–600 words]

Describe the methods in two parts:
1. **Computational methods**: What algorithms, tools, and parameters? What will they detect or measure? Cite foundational method papers.
2. **Interpretive methods**: How will computational findings be interpreted? What theoretical framework, if any?

Address OCR quality and its impact on the methodology if working with digitized historical sources.

---

## Ethical Considerations

[100–200 words]

Address:
- Copyright and licensing of primary materials
- Data consent requirements (if any living subjects or contemporary social media)
- Known biases in the corpus and how they will be named/handled
- Anonymization if relevant
