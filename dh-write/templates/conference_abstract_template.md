## Abstract

**[Opening paragraph — The Humanistic Problem]** (~80–100 words)

Introduce the research problem in humanistic terms. What are you studying, and why does it matter? Open with the object of study (a corpus, a cultural phenomenon, a historical period), not with a general claim about digital humanities. State the research question.

*Example opening*: "This paper examines [specific corpus/phenomenon], asking [specific question]. Prior scholarship has [established X / left Y unresolved]. We argue that [main claim], using [method] to [how the method answers the question]."

---

**[Middle paragraphs — Corpus, Method, Findings]** (~200–250 words)

**Corpus**: Describe the primary sources in 2–3 sentences. Include size, date range, archive source, and any known data quality issues. If working with digitized historical materials, mention OCR quality or preprocessing.

**Method**: Name the computational or interpretive method. Explain *why* this method is suited to this question — the method-question fit. Cite the foundational method paper(s) by author-date. Name the tools used.

**Findings / Argument**: State the main finding or argument in 3–5 sentences. What did the analysis reveal? How does this finding support the interpretive claim? For papers with close reading components, mention how the computational and interpretive analyses interact.

*This section is where most conference abstracts fail: they describe the method but bury or omit the finding. State the finding and its humanistic significance explicitly.*

---

**[Closing paragraph — Contribution]** (~80–100 words)

What does this paper contribute to:
1. The humanistic field (literary studies / history / linguistics / cultural studies)?
2. DH methodology (what does this application of the method add to the tool's use in DH)?
3. The conference theme (if a specific theme)?

State the contribution in direct, jargon-minimal language — conference abstract reviewers often include non-DH specialists.
