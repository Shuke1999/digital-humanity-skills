# DH Review Criteria

## Overview

DH papers must satisfy criteria from two scholarly traditions. Unlike STEM peer review (which evaluates methods, results, and significance) or humanities review (which evaluates argument, evidence, and scholarly engagement), DH review requires both.

---

## The Six Core DH Review Criteria

### 1. Research Question Quality
- Is the research question specific and answerable?
- Is it dual-framed: does it have both an interpretive claim and a methodological contribution?
- Is the question humanistically significant — does it matter for the understanding of culture, history, or literature?
- Is it genuinely new, or has it been answered by prior scholarship?

### 2. Corpus Integrity
- Is the corpus appropriate for the research question (right time period, geography, source type)?
- Are construction decisions (inclusions, exclusions) documented and justified?
- Are known biases named and assessed?
- For digitized historical sources: is OCR quality addressed?
- Are rights and access constraints handled appropriately?

### 3. Methodological Soundness
- Does the method fit the research question (method-question fit)?
- Is the method described with enough precision to be reproducible?
- Are tools cited?
- Are parameter choices explained (not just reported)?
- Are method limitations acknowledged?

### 4. Interpretive Depth
- Are computational findings interpreted, not just reported?
- Does the discussion connect the computation to the humanistic argument?
- Is there close reading or qualitative engagement to ground the aggregate findings?
- Does the paper avoid "method imperialism" — using computation to produce findings that displace rather than support humanistic reasoning?

### 5. Scholarly Engagement
- Is relevant prior scholarship cited and engaged (not just listed)?
- Does the paper situate its contribution in the existing conversation?
- Are competing interpretations addressed?
- Is the paper's novelty supported by the literature review?

### 6. Clarity and Communication
- Is the research question stated clearly?
- Is the argument developed progressively?
- Can a humanist follow the computational sections? Can a computer scientist follow the interpretive sections?
- Are field-specific terms defined for the broader DH audience?
- Is the writing quality appropriate for the target venue?

## DH-Specific Review Criteria (Beyond Standard Academic Review)

These criteria are unique to DH and often not captured by generic review rubrics:

| Criterion | Question |
|---|---|
| **The DH bridge** | Does the paper justify using DH methods for this question? Could this be done with traditional methods? If so, why DH? |
| **Reproducibility** | Are data and code available (or planned)? DH community increasingly expects this. |
| **Corpus as scholarly artifact** | Does the paper treat corpus construction as a scholarly decision, not an administrative task? |
| **Scale-interpretation mismatch** | Does the paper make claims at a scale (e.g. all Victorian novels) that the corpus doesn't support? |
| **OCR opacity** | Does the paper use digitized historical text without disclosing data quality? |
| **Tool citation** | Are all analytical software tools cited as scholarly instruments? |

## Scoring Scale (for Structured Review)

For each of the six core criteria + DH-specific criteria:

| Score | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 5 — Excellent | No issues; meets or exceeds standards |
| 4 — Good | Minor issues; easily addressed |
| 3 — Adequate | Some issues; revision required |
| 2 — Needs Work | Significant issues; major revision |
| 1 — Insufficient | Does not meet minimum standard for this criterion |

**Overall recommendation thresholds**:
- Average 4.5+: Accept with minor revisions
- Average 3.5–4.4: Minor revisions
- Average 2.5–3.4: Major revisions
- Average below 2.5: Reject / revise and resubmit
